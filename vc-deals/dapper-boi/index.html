§ 01
Executive Summary

The thesis in one paragraph

What you'd say if the partner stopped you in the hallway.

Dapper Boi is a gender-neutral apparel brand founded 2015 by Vicky Pasche, with a real product story (chest-gap snaps, deep pockets, extended sizing across body types), genuine repeat behavior (DECK 51% repeat vs. ~25% DTC benchmark), and credible media validation (Inc Female Founder 2025, Tory Burch Foundation Fellow 2025, CBS Mornings). The team has compounded $6.7M cumulative lifetime revenue on $1.75M cumulative capital — that is genuinely capital-efficient for DTC apparel. The bull case is a "Levi's of inclusive apparel" wedge into a real, underserved $100B+ gender-neutral + workwear market. The bear case — and the one we are unable to dismiss — is that after ten years the business is at $1.4M ARR, the $6.7M "run rate" figure in the deck reconciles only to cumulative lifetime revenue not annualized, the company is now projecting 72× growth in six years from that base on a sub-$1M round, and after a decade of operations no tier-1 institutional investor has led a priced round. The product and founder are real; the venture math is not yet earned.

The deck is selling a venture-scale hockey stick. The financials describe a capital-efficient bootstrapped lifestyle brand. Those are different businesses, and we cannot underwrite both at once.

What We Like

  • 51% repeat rate — 2× DTC average, signals real customer love
  • Capital efficiency: $6.7M lifetime rev on $1.75M raised (≈3.8× capital efficiency)
  • Founder grit + media validation (Inc, Tory Burch, CBS)
  • Genuine product differentiation (chest-gap snaps proprietary)
  • Real underserved category — gender-neutral + extended sizing is a structural gap

What Concerns Us

  • 10 years to $1.4M revenue — growth rate inconsistent with venture math
  • "$6.7M run rate" claim does not reconcile with stated 2024 revenue of $1.4M
  • $1M raise after 10 yrs implies no prior tier-1 lead — signal, not noise
  • 72× growth to $101M by 2030 from $1.4M base requires four concurrent strategies (DTC + Retail + Enterprise + Geo)
  • Spain HQ for primarily US-targeted brand — adds tax/ops complexity
  • Enterprise workwear is a different business, not a line extension

Recommended Next Step

Do not commit until founder reconciles the "$6.7M run rate" claim against 2024 reported revenue of $1.4M, provides monthly revenue and cohort retention data for the last 24 months, and explains the 10-year revenue curve. If reconciliation holds and 2025 YTD is tracking the $3M projection, revisit as a $50–150K check at the back of a syndicate, not a leading position. Decision-ready in 4–6 weeks pending data-room.

§ 03
Traction & Capital Efficiency

Point 1: Capital-efficient brand-building, slow-growing business

The repeat rate is real. The growth rate is not yet venture-scale.

The first thing to like about Dapper Boi is that it has compounded into a real brand on very little capital. $1.75M raised over a decade producing $6.7M in cumulative lifetime revenue is a capital efficiency multiple of ~3.8× — well above the DTC apparel cohort, which typically burns multiples of revenue to acquire customers. The 51% repeat rate is the metric that justifies the bull case: customers who buy once come back, and that is the precondition for a brand business that compounds rather than churns.

But the second thing to notice is that this capital efficiency has not yet translated into a growth rate that warrants a venture round. The reported sequence is DECK 2022 $600K, 2023 $900K (+50%), 2024 $1.4M (+55%). That's strong percentage growth on a small base but absolute growth of $500K/year, with cumulative lifetime revenue of $6.7M after ten years. The deck's projection then steps to $3M (2025), $10.8M (2026), $30.1M (2027), $61.3M (2028), $101.3M (2030) — implying a sustained 100%+ CAGR that the company has never demonstrated.

Why It Matters

The 51% repeat rate is the single most important data point in this memo. It is the only metric that distinguishes Dapper Boi from a long tail of bootstrapped DTC apparel businesses that stall sub-$5M. If the repeat rate holds at 51% in cohorts of 5K+ customers (not just the early adopter base), the brand has the customer economics to compound. If it degrades materially in later cohorts — which is the typical DTC pattern as you acquire less-loyal traffic — the entire thesis collapses, because everything else (workwear, retail, geo) depends on a profitable DTC engine.

§ 04
Brand & Product Moat

The thing that's actually defensible

Not the snaps. The customer relationship.

Functional product details — deep pockets, chest-gap snaps, extended-sizing fit blocks — are improvable but not defensible. A well-capitalized incumbent (Levi's, Madewell, Old Navy) could engineer around all three in a season. The same is true of the size-inclusive positioning: Universal Standard, Old Navy's Bodequality, and others have shown that mass brands can expand size ranges when they choose to.

What is defensible — if it holds — is the relationship between Dapper Boi and a specific customer who has been ignored by mainstream apparel for a long time. The 51% repeat rate, the social following, the media coverage (Inc, Forbes, Out100), and the founder's personal authority within the community are all expressions of one underlying asset: a brand that is trusted by an audience that does not trust mass apparel brands. Replicating that trust is what an incumbent cannot do quickly with a product line extension.

What's in the Layer Today

  • 51% repeat purchase rate
  • 158K+ social followers
  • Earned media: Inc, Forbes, Entrepreneur, Out100, CBS Mornings
  • Founder credibility (Tory Burch Fellow, Inc Female Founder 2025)
  • Functional product distinction (snaps, pockets, fit)
  • Nordstrom wholesale relationship (real, growing)

What It Could Become

  • Category-defining brand for inclusive fit (the "Levi's of…" framing)
  • Multi-channel: DTC anchor + curated wholesale + B2B workwear
  • Geographic platform: US/UK/EU/AU/CA with one supply chain
  • Acquisition target for a mainstream player needing inclusive credibility
  • Adjacent line extensions: footwear, accessories, kids (Phase 4)
The IP isn't the snap. The IP is being the first brand a queer/non-binary customer thinks of when they need jeans that fit. That asset is real — and it took ten years to build. The question is whether $1M is enough capital to defend it before a better-funded entrant decides to compete.
§ 05
Commercial & Channel Footprint

Point 2: Three channels, three margin profiles, one team

DTC pays the bills. Retail builds the brand. Enterprise workwear is the call option — and the focus risk.

Dapper Boi is pursuing three distinct go-to-market motions simultaneously: a DTC e-commerce engine (64%+ gross margin), a curated wholesale program (Nordstrom + Wolf & Badger, 55% margin), and a planned enterprise workwear vertical targeting hospitality and similar uniform contracts (68% margin). The case for doing all three is that they share a product base, a brand, and a supply chain. The case against is that each has different sales motions, customer cycles, and operational requirements — and the team is small enough that pursuing all three could slow each.

Why This Is the Thesis
  • Three margin profiles diversify revenue resilience (DTC volatility offset by wholesale + enterprise recurring)
  • Enterprise workwear at 68% GM with contract revenue is the highest-quality dollar in the model
  • Nordstrom relationship is already producing — the wholesale motion is proven, not theoretical
  • Hotel workwear pipeline is a credible adjacency (gender-neutral uniforms = real procurement demand)
Open Questions
  • What is the current channel mix by % of revenue? Deck does not break it out.
  • How many enterprise workwear contracts are signed vs. in pipeline? Deck implies "pipeline" only.
  • Nordstrom doors / SKUs / sell-through — undisclosed
  • Wolf & Badger contribution — onboarding, not yet revenue-generating
  • Does the team have B2B sales experience for enterprise workwear, or is this an "if we hire it" plan?

§ 06
Funding & Cap Structure

The 10-year, $1.75M signal

What the cap table tells you that the deck doesn't.

Dapper Boi has raised $1.75M cumulative across ten years. That number is doing two jobs in the deck — it is presented (correctly) as evidence of capital efficiency, and it is presented (less explicitly) as the runway that funded $6.7M of lifetime revenue. We grant the capital efficiency. We are more cautious about the second framing.

A decade of operations producing a maximum total raise of $1.75M, and no priced round led by a tier-1 institutional investor that we have been able to identify, is a signal. Founders who raise from tier-1 firms typically do so because the firms write the check that scales the business; founders who do not, often have not been able to. The deck does not name the prior backers. This needs to be verified — either the absence of a tier-1 in the cap table is an unambiguous historical signal, or there is a name we have not yet found.

Capital efficiency is admirable when the strategic choice is "stay lean." It is a constraint when the strategic intent is "scale to $100M revenue." Dapper Boi has now declared the latter intent and is raising $1M to execute it. Those two numbers — the goal and the round — do not match.
