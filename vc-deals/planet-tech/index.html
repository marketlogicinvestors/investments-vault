The Thesis in One Paragraph

What you'd say if the partner stopped you in the hallway.

PLANET.TECH is a pre-revenue angel bet on a $35-COGS cattle health collar that claims a sensor capability no competitor offers — pulse oximetry through hide — distributed through veterinarians into a cattle health market that sits at the epicenter of a real, government-confirmed biosecurity crisis, at a $6M post-money cap that is 333× cheaper than the category's most recently validated comparable.

The company is run by a door-to-door sales founder selling farm-to-farm, a two-time biomedical engineer CTO, and an aerospace engineer VP Engineering — an unconventional team that nobody inside ag would have assembled, which is partly why it might work. The $500K angel round exists to ship 1,000 collars by July 2026, sign paying beta farms, and fund a 100-cow Texas A&M pilot in September 2026, then open a priced pre-seed "from proof, not promises." The entry price is attractive. The core risk is hardware and clinical validation: if the pulse-ox-through-hide signal is noisy or clinically useless in real cattle environments, the differentiation collapses. The committee votes on whether the four diligence gates below — sensor data, verified customer pipeline, manufacturing BOM, and vet channel proof — can be cleared before close.

What We Like
  • The New World Screwworm outbreak has closed the US–Mexico cattle border for 12+ months — government-confirmed, independently verified urgency, not deck-manufactured.[1][2]
  • Only cattle collar on the market claiming pulse oximetry. No competitor — Merck/Allflex, CowManager, smaXtec, or Halter — offers heart rate and blood oxygen through hide.[3]
  • Halter's $220M Series E at $2B valuation led by Founders Fund (March 2026) proves the category and the exit ceiling. PLANET.TECH enters at $6M cap — 333× discount to the validated comparable.[4]
  • $35 COGS, if verified, is the lowest in the category and directly attacks the primary adoption barrier: upfront hardware cost.
  • Vet channel model is the most original GTM insight in the market — routes around the high-CAC, farm-by-farm direct-sales trap that kills most ag-tech hardware.
  • Dual-product architecture: Mootality provides the health wedge; Moola monetizes methane from the same collar platform — one CAC, two revenue streams.
What Concerns Us
  • Entire deal is pre-revenue. "17K heads signed up" and "46 farms / 106,000 cattle" are company-provided, unaudited, and contractually undefined. Signed-up is not the same as contracted or paid.
  • Pulse oximetry through bovine hide under motion, mud, rain, temperature extremes, and variable pigmentation is hard. The "BPM 86→112" slide is an anecdote, not validation data. Clinical usefulness is unproven.
  • $35 COGS is likely a component estimate, not fully-loaded. Connectivity, battery, NRE, fulfillment, warranty, and yield assumptions are missing.
  • 8-person equity-only team is high-conviction but fragile — no cash runway buffer and no deep ag-industry, regulatory, or supply-chain expertise evident on the deck.
  • Moola / methane capture is a speculative second act. Enteric capture from free-ranging cattle via wearable is unproven at commercial scale and depends on volatile carbon credit and LCFS policy.
  • Halter is actively expanding into the US market — the "NZ only" framing in the deck is stale.[4]

§ 03

The Pulse Oximetry Wedge

From sensor claim to clinical outcome — the make-or-break diligence question.

The company's central differentiation claim is that no competitor offers pulse oximetry on a cattle collar. That categorical claim is independently corroborated: a review of Merck/Allflex SenseHub, CowManager, smaXtec, and Halter confirms that current cattle-monitoring products rely on body temperature (infrared thermistor), activity (accelerometer), rumination, and behavioral inference — not direct blood-oxygenation measurement.[3][5] The deck's competitive table correctly identifies this gap.

The critical unknown is whether the pulse-ox signal is clinically useful, not merely technically novel. The deck shows a single data point: BPM 86 → 112 on Day 01, Test 01. That is an anecdote, not a validation study. The questions the committee needs answered before advancing capital are:

Core Sensor Validation Questions

  • What is the measurement accuracy for heart rate and SpO2 across multiple breeds, hide colors, hair lengths, and movement states compared to a veterinary-grade reference device? DILIGENCE GATE 1 — must be cleared before close
  • How does signal quality degrade under real-field conditions: mud, rain, cold, heat, collar fit variation, sweating, and running? Hardware / sensor robustness
  • What is the detection sensitivity and specificity for each of the 17 targeted disease conditions? What are the false-positive and false-negative rates? Clinical utility threshold
  • What is the measured early-detection lead time versus visual inspection and activity-only systems? Core value proposition proof
  • What is the Texas A&M P1 pilot protocol and what specific endpoints will it measure? Structured validation catalyst

The Pricing Engine analogy from Intellectible applies here: in that deal, production status at named accounts was the most important update. For PLANET.TECH, the equivalent inflection point is Texas A&M pilot data showing signal accuracy and disease detection lead time versus a control group. That is the single most important near-term catalyst for this investment — and why the right posture is a conditional small check now rather than a full allocation before data.

§ 04

The Competitive Advantage

Why this platform is potentially hard to replicate — and what must be proven.

PLANET.TECH's defensibility rests on three layers, in descending order of verifiability:

  1. Sensor IP — Pulse Oximetry Through Bovine Hide. Seven patents filed covering the sensing method. If the method works and the patents are defensible, this creates a genuine technical moat that cannot be copied by a software update to an existing platform. The moat depends entirely on patent claim scope and sensor accuracy validation. Request patent application numbers and claim summaries.
  2. Per-Animal Biological Data Moat. On-device AI establishes individual baselines per cow. As the collar population grows, PLANET.TECH accumulates the largest and most granular cattle biometric dataset in existence. This creates compounding switching costs — a farmer who leaves loses years of individualized baseline data — and creates a training dataset for disease-detection models that new entrants cannot replicate quickly.
  3. Vet Channel Lock-In. If veterinary practices adopt the multi-farm command center, they become embedded in the distribution and workflow layer. A vet who onboards 50 farms to PLANET.TECH's platform has strong economic and operational incentive to stay. This is a network-effect distribution moat, not a product moat — it depends on execution, not IP.
  4. Cost Structure Advantage. At $35 COGS (if verified), PLANET.TECH has structural room to price aggressively against $75–175/head incumbents while maintaining margin. This advantage is real only if the BOM holds at production volumes, including connectivity, battery, warranty, and NRE amortization.

Go-to-Market: The Vet Channel

The most original distribution insight in the deck — and the most important unproven thesis.

Channel thesis: There are approximately 2,500 veterinary practices in the US, each managing health across 50–100 farms. PLANET.TECH offers vets a multi-farm command center at $1/head/month — no upfront cost, pure margin for the vet — making each vet practice a warm-introduction funnel to 50+ farms. One vet adoption equals 50+ farm warm intros, compressing the cost and time of farm-by-farm direct sales.

Land Motion

  • CEO sells farm-to-farm directly as primary near-term GTM — top-2% nationally in door-to-door sales
  • Target beachhead: California (2.4M cattle, $288M TAM) as initial geography
  • Direct approach to farm operators; complement with vet-referred intros
  • $10/head/month subscription; 5-week payback claim must be validated with ROI bridge

Expand Motion via Vets

  • Vet platform at $1/head/month — no upfront cost, pure margin for practice
  • No multi-farm vet command center exists anywhere today — claimed whitespace
  • One vet practice = access to 50–100 farms; 2,500 practices nationally
  • Vet endorsement converts cold farm sale into trusted referral with clinical champion

Diligence Gate 3 requires evidence that at least one veterinary practice has signed a partner agreement, understands the economics, and has begun using or agreed to pilot the multi-farm command center. The vet channel model is compelling in structure but has zero demonstrated traction. Without a live vet partner, it remains a go-to-market hypothesis — and the company's entire low-CAC thesis depends on it.

§ 08

Market Context

TAM, the screwworm crisis, and structural opportunity.

Market framing: The US cattle herd stood at approximately 86.2 million head as of January 1, 2026, according to USDA's Cattle Inventory report — in a contraction phase with limited opportunity for meaningful expansion before at least 2028.[7] PLANET.TECH's $11.3B US health TAM is based on $10/head/month × 94M cattle — a slightly overstated herd count, and a price point at the top of the wearable price band. A more conservative SAM using $60/head/year (midpoint of current installed-product pricing) and 10% penetration of the US herd implies an initial addressable market of approximately $500M.[6]

Structural Tailwinds

  • New World Screwworm crisis is real and government-confirmed. As of May 29, 2026, Reuters reported NWS detected 31 miles from the US border in Coahuila, Mexico.[1] The US southern border has been closed to cattle imports for over 12 months.[2] USDA is still only weighing a phased reopening. This is the highest urgency cattle health context in 60 years and directly validates early-detection demand.
  • Global dairy cattle disease losses estimated at $65B annually, with $8B in annual losses in the US alone — dominated by mastitis, ketosis, and lameness.[8] This is independently peer-reviewed economic burden, not deck-constructed TAM.
  • Precision livestock farming is growing but adoption is uneven. USDA/NIFA identifies initial cost, uncertain ROI, and technology complexity as the primary adoption barriers.[9] A verified 5-week payback would directly address all three — which is why that claim is the most commercially important number in the deck.
  • Category validated by Halter's $220M Series E at $2B. Founders Fund, Bessemer, DCVC, Bond, and Blackbird all made major bets on cattle-collar AI in March 2026.[4] That capital formation proves both market size and exit ceiling.
Comparable category outcomes: Halter at $2B (virtual fencing + AI collars, cattle) is the on-point comparable. PLANET.TECH is the analogous platform for cattle health biometrics — with a broader sensor stack and a second-act climate monetization layer that Halter does not have.

§ 10

The Moola / Methane Option

Extraordinary upside, extraordinary claims — underwrite as a free call option only.

PLANET.TECH's second-act vision converts the same collar hardware into a methane-capture device, generating $400–600/cow/year in new farmer revenue from renewable natural gas, carbon credits, or sustainable aviation fuel. Livestock emissions are a documented large problem: FAO estimates livestock supply chains account for approximately 12–14.5% of global anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions, with cattle responsible for roughly 62% of that.[10][11] The commercial opportunity in monetizing those emissions is real — California dairy digesters have demonstrated 80% methane capture from manure lagoons.[12]

The critical distinction: economically proven methane capture operates on manure lagoons (point-source, stationary), not on individual free-roaming cattle via wearables. Capturing enteric methane (from cow burps) via a collar on a grazing animal is a fundamentally different and far harder engineering problem. The company's LinkedIn posts claim 34% field capture efficiency with a jump from 3% to 20.8% in six weeks as of late 2025 — an extraordinary claim that has not been independently validated or published.

For IC purposes: Moola is a free call option on a potentially category-defining climate outcome. If it works, PLANET.TECH becomes a planetary-scale company, not just a cattle-health SaaS. If it fails, the base case — Mootality as a billion-dollar cattle health platform — still stands. Do not let Moola either carry or kill the investment thesis.

Additional risk: the revenue economics of Moola depend on California LCFS credit pricing, voluntary carbon markets, and potential regulatory changes. California lawmakers have already moved to eliminate "avoided methane credits" for animal agriculture through A.B. 2870 (did not proceed in 2024 but signals political risk).[13] Do not model Moola revenue until (a) capture technology is validated at scale and (b) a buyer or off-take agreement exists.

At $120/head/year subscription, gross margin compresses to software-adjacent levels. Hardware cash cycle creates a working capital crunch before the pre-seed closes.
  • Halter adds biometrics in its next collar generation (12–24 months). With $220M in capital and 1M+ collars deployed globally, Halter can commoditize PLANET.TECH's sensor advantage. The window to lock in distribution and data moat is shorter than the deck implies.
  • Moola methane capture remains commercially unviable. Carbon and LCFS credit pricing drops. The climate story distracts the team from the harder but more immediate work of proving the health collar. $500K burns faster than expected on dual-product development. The pre-seed does not materialize on favorable terms.
