Executive Summary

The thesis in one paragraph: what the market is missing

Energy Transfer is the infrastructure bet on American energy supremacy: the toll-road operator for every molecule of natural gas, NGL, crude oil, and refined product that moves through the most prolific production basins in the United States. At $19.10 per unit, it trades at 8.4x 2026E EBITDA against peers at 9.7–11.2x, yielding 7.2% with 1.72x distribution coverage. The discount reflects a founder-controlled MLP governance structure, a 2020 distribution cut, and a Lake Charles LNG reversal. What the market is not pricing: AI/data center gas demand inflection (three named contracts, ~$440M unmodeled EBITDA), the $13–21B of ET-entity acquisition headroom, and a succession catalyst that could close 1–2 turns of multiple in a single announcement.

What we like
  • Fee-based fortress. 90%+ of segment margin fee-based. Volume is the only sensitivity: not price. Take-or-pay and MVC contracts across gathering, transport, and fractionation.
  • AI demand tailwind: three named contracts. Meta Hyperion (Tiger Pipeline, 250k MMBtu/d, 20-year, Feb 2028). CloudBurst (Oasis Pipeline, 450k MMBtu/d). Oracle (~900 MMcf/d, active). ~$440M of annual EBITDA not in consensus.
  • Desert Southwest capital efficiency. Phase 2 upsized: +0.8 Bcf/d for only $900M additional = 4.2x build multiple vs. 6x headline. Market is modeling the wrong return on ET's best project.
  • Valuation gap. 2-turn discount on $18.4B EBITDA = ~$36.8B of implied market cap gap. At 3.43B units: ~$10.70/unit of trapped value = 56% of current price.
  • Canadian crude hedge. Southern Illinois Connector (100 MBpd, 2028) and DAPL Phase 2 (250 MBpd, 2029+) provide volume floor independent of Permian drilling.
What concerns us
  • The DCF leak. EBITDA grew 3% in 2025. DCF fell $8.4B → $8.2B. Interest expense absorbed the entire operating improvement: up $600M to $4.2B.
  • Governance discount is structural. GP control locks out ~40% of institutional buyer universe permanently until C-Corp conversion. The 2020 Preferred Unit controversy is in institutional memory.
  • Subsidiaries at leverage ceiling. SUN at 4.0x post-Parkland. USAC at 4.0x post-J-W Power. USAC equity is negative (−$112M). Only ET entity has deal capacity.
  • Capex execution risk. Desert Southwest at $5.6B, Q4 2029. ET history includes a $3.3B Rover Pipeline write-down. 18-month slip = $800M–$1.2B 2027 EBITDA miss.
  • Succession unpriced. Marshall McCrea retiring by Dec 31 2026. No successor named. Single biggest unpriced event in ET's near-term trajectory.

§ GL

Glossary of Terms

Every unit, abbreviation, and metric used in this memo: expanded

Volume & flow units

BTUBritish Thermal Unit. The base energy unit. Amount of heat needed to raise 1 pound of water by 1°F. Everything in natural gas is measured in multiples of BTU.
MMBtuOne Million BTU. The standard trading unit for natural gas. Roughly equivalent to 1,000 cubic feet (1 Mcf) of gas at standard conditions. When ET charges a tariff of $0.75/MMBtu, it earns $0.75 for every million BTUs transported.
MMBtu/dMillion BTU per day. Daily flow rate. The Meta Hyperion deal: 250,000 MMBtu/d = 250 million BTUs flowing through Tiger Pipeline every single day for 20 years.
McfOne Thousand cubic feet of natural gas. Standard field measurement. 1 Mcf ≈ 1 MMBtu (the exact ratio depends on gas energy content, but they are used interchangeably in modelling).
MMcfOne Million cubic feet. 1,000 Mcf. Oracle’s deal: ~900 MMcf/d means 900 million cubic feet of gas per day flowing to Oracle data centers.
MMcf/dMillion cubic feet per day. Daily pipeline flow rate. The Oracle deal at 900 MMcf/d is the same as 0.9 Bcf/d. Used interchangeably with MMBtu/d in ET filings.
BcfOne Billion cubic feet of natural gas. 1,000 MMcf. The unit used for large pipeline capacities. Desert Southwest at 2.3 Bcf/d carries 2.3 billion cubic feet of gas every day: enough to supply roughly 23 million homes.
Bcf/dBillion cubic feet per day. The primary capacity unit for major interstate pipelines. Hugh Brinson: 2.2 Bcf/d eastbound. Desert Southwest: 2.3 Bcf/d total. Oracle: 0.9 Bcf/d. Higher Bcf/d = bigger pipe, more tariff revenue.
TcfOne Trillion cubic feet. 1,000 Bcf. Used for annual production or reserve estimates at the national level. US dry gas production is roughly 100 Bcf/d ≈ 36.5 Tcf/yr. Rarely used at the project level.
MBpdThousand barrels per day. Used for crude oil and NGLs. Southern Illinois Connector: 100 MBpd of Canadian crude. DAPL Phase 2: 250 MBpd. One barrel = 42 US gallons.
MMBpdMillion barrels per day. National-scale crude oil flow. US crude production is roughly 13 MMBpd. ET’s crude segment moves hundreds of MBpd, not millions: but still among the largest single-operator crude networks.
Horsepower (HP)Compression power unit. USA Compression (USAC) operates compressors measured in HP. J-W Power added 0.8 million active HP. More HP = more compression capacity = more gas moved through gathering pipes at higher pressure.

Financial metrics

EBITDAEarnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation & Amortization. ET’s primary operating performance metric. Strips out capital structure and accounting choices. 2025A: $15.98B. The number Bramhall leads every earnings call with.
Adj. EBITDAAdjusted EBITDA. EBITDA further adjusted to remove one-time items (transaction costs, integration charges, mark-to-market gains/losses). ET uses Adj. EBITDA as its official guidance metric. Always use Adj. EBITDA when comparing to guidance.
DCFDistributable Cash Flow. The cash available to pay unitholders after operating expenses, interest, maintenance capex, and preferred distributions. ET’s 2025A DCF: $8.2B. The number that determines whether the distribution is safe. DCF ÷ distributions = coverage ratio.
Coverage ratioDCF ÷ total distributions paid. ET’s kill switch: 1.5x. At 1.5x, ET generates $1.50 of DCF for every $1.00 paid in distributions. Below 1.5x for two consecutive quarters = distribution sustainability question. 2025A: 1.67x. 2026E: 1.72x.
MVCMinimum Volume Commitment. A contract clause requiring a shipper to pay a deficiency fee if it ships less than a contractually agreed minimum. ET’s primary protection against volume risk. If a producer drills less, they still pay the MVC deficiency.
EV / EBITDAEnterprise Value divided by EBITDA. The primary valuation multiple for infrastructure. ET: 8.4x. Peers: 9.7–11.2x. A higher multiple means the market pays more per dollar of EBITDA. ET’s 2-turn discount = the market pays ~$25B less than peers for the same cash flow.
Build multipleCapex ÷ annual EBITDA generated. How much ET spends to build $1 of annual EBITDA. Desert Southwest Phase 1: $4.7B capex ÷ $783M EBITDA = 6.0x. Phase 2: $900M ÷ $214M = 4.2x. Lower is better. ET targets sub-6x across its growth capex portfolio.
IDRIncentive Distribution Right. A legacy MLP feature giving the General Partner an escalating share of distributions as they grow. ET eliminated its IDRs in 2018. Sunoco LP IDRs are owned by ET. Historically, IDRs transferred value from limited partners to the GP: their elimination was a structural improvement for unitholders.
K-1IRS Schedule K-1. The tax form MLP unitholders receive instead of a 1099. Requires different tax treatment, delays filing season, and is incompatible with most IRAs and many institutional mandates. The K-1 requirement is a structural reason why ∼40% of institutional capital cannot hold ET units: contributing directly to the valuation discount.
GP / LPGeneral Partner / Limited Partner. MLP ownership structure. The GP controls the partnership and its management. Kelcy Warren controls ET’s GP. LP unitholders (public investors) own economic rights but limited governance rights. This is the governance discount in three letters.

Infrastructure & pipeline terms

GatheringCollecting gas from wellheads. The first stage of the midstream chain. ET’s WTG acquisition added 6,000 miles of Midland Basin gathering lines. Gathering pipes are smaller-diameter, shorter-distance, and carry lower pressures than interstate transmission pipes.
ProcessingSeparating gas components. Raw wellhead gas contains methane (dry gas) plus heavier hydrocarbons (NGLs). Processing plants separate them. ET’s Mustang Draw facilities process Permian gas before it enters the interstate system. ET earns a per-MMBtu processing fee.
NGLNatural Gas Liquids. The heavier components extracted during gas processing: ethane, propane, butane, isobutane, and natural gasoline. NGLs are transported as liquids in separate pipelines and fractionated at Mont Belvieu. ET’s NGL & refined products segment: $4.15B EBITDA in 2025A: the largest single segment.
FractionationSeparating the NGL mix into individual components. Mixed NGL liquid (y-grade) arrives at Mont Belvieu and is fractured into its individual components by boiling point. ET operates one of the world’s largest fractionation complexes. Charges a per-barrel fee regardless of the price of the resulting products.
FERCFederal Energy Regulatory Commission. The US regulator governing interstate natural gas pipelines. FERC sets the maximum tariff ET can charge on interstate pipes using a cost-of-service methodology that allows ET a ~10–12% regulated return on invested capital. More pipeline under FERC = more predictable revenue.
Take-or-payContract requiring payment whether or not volumes flow. The strongest form of volume protection. A producer commits to minimum throughput and pays even if they drill less. ET’s gathering and processing contracts are predominantly take-or-pay. This is the reason “fee-based” is meaningful rather than just marketing language.
Behind-the-meterGas delivered directly to a facility, bypassing the utility grid. CloudBurst’s data center receives gas directly from the Oasis Pipeline rather than through a local distribution company. Allows higher volumes, faster permitting, and avoids utility rate cases. ET earns the full tariff without sharing with a utility intermediary.
DAPLDakota Access Pipeline. ET owns 36.4% of this 1,172-mile crude oil pipeline from the Bakken formation in North Dakota to Patoka, Illinois. Controversial at construction (Standing Rock). Now one of the most efficient Bakken crude takeaway routes. Phase 2 would add Canadian crude volumes from Enbridge’s Main Line system.

Binary 04

Interest expense stabilizes as the capex cycle peaks

The DCF leak assumption

The assumption: ET’s interest expense stabilizes at approximately $4.2–4.6B as growth capex peaks in 2026–2027 and existing debt matures at refinancing rates that do not materially exceed the current blended cost of ~5.2%. DCF conversion recovers from the 51.3% trough in 2025 back toward 55%+ by 2027.

What breaks if wrong: Every $500M of additional interest expense = $500M of DCF compression, with zero EBITDA offset. If interest grows from $4.2B to $4.7B in 2026, coverage drops to approximately 1.62x. At $5.2B, coverage approaches the 1.5x kill switch. The DCF leak that started in 2025 becomes structural, not cyclical.

Signal to watch:

  • The single most important line item in ET’s quarterly P&L: interest expense. Q1 2026 annualized run-rate is $4.2B. If Q2 2026 shows sequential increase, the inflection assumption is wrong.

The MLP Structure: Why It Exists, Why ET Keeps It, and Why Peers Are Moving Away

The legal and economic logic that built the midstream industry. The structural cost that now burdens it.

What an MLP is and why it was invented

A Master Limited Partnership is a publicly traded limited partnership that combines the tax efficiency of a partnership (pass-through taxation, no entity-level corporate income tax) with the liquidity of a publicly listed security. Congress created the qualifying income test in 1987 under IRC Section 7704(d): to qualify as an MLP, at least 90% of gross income must come from "qualifying sources," which include natural gas pipelines, storage, processing, and transportation of oil and gas products. [Inferred from tax law history]

The core economic logic: Because an MLP pays no corporate income tax, it can distribute a larger share of operating cash flow to unitholders than an equivalent C-Corp paying a 21% federal rate. At $18B of EBITDA, the difference between a 21% corporate tax rate and 0% is approximately $2-3B per year in distributable cash available. That cash either goes to the government (C-Corp) or to unitholders (MLP). In 1987 through roughly 2014, MLP investors were predominantly US retail and institutional investors who preferred current income over deferred capital gains. The structure served them.

Why ET adopted it: Energy Transfer Company was formed in 1996 as a propane pipeline partnership. When it went public in 2004 as Energy Transfer Partners, the MLP structure was the dominant model for midstream infrastructure capital formation. Every major midstream company went public as an MLP in the 2000s. The alternative, a C-Corp, would have paid corporate income tax on the same cash flows, reducing the distributable income available to investors and increasing the effective cost of equity capital. [Inferred from ET formation history]

The MLP structure: what it gives and what it costs

What the MLP structure gives

  • No entity-level corporate tax. Taxable income passes through to unitholders. At $18B of EBITDA, that is a multi-billion-dollar structural tax advantage annually vs. an equivalent C-Corp.
  • Pass-through depreciation and depletion. Unitholders receive allocated deductions (including large accelerated depreciation on infrastructure assets) that can offset ordinary income. In early years, most MLP distributions are classified as non-taxable return of capital, deferring the tax event until the unit is sold.
  • Access to yield-focused capital. The 7%+ yield attracts income-oriented retail and institutional investors who need current income. This is a specific and large investor base that would not hold a 3-4% yielding C-Corp for the same asset quality.
  • Acquisition currency. MLP units can be issued to acquire assets or companies at lower effective cost than C-Corp equity when the MLP is trading at a premium to NAV. All-unit transactions like the Crestwood deal are funded at the MLP's cost of equity, not the corporate tax-burdened cost. [Inferred]

What the MLP structure costs

  • K-1 tax complexity. Unitholders receive Schedule K-1 (not 1099-DIV) forms reporting their allocated share of partnership income, deductions, and credits. K-1s are complex, often arrive late (March-April), may require multi-state filings, and create UBTI risk in IRAs and tax-exempt accounts. This eliminates approximately 40-50% of the institutional investor universe that cannot hold K-1 issuers.
  • S&P 500 and major index exclusion. MLPs are not included in the S&P 500 or most broad equity indexes. The four midstream C-Corps in the S&P 500 (KMI, ONEOK, TRGP, WMB) receive passive index buying from every S&P 500 index fund. ET does not. This structural demand difference creates a persistent valuation gap. [Ext: ETFTrends Alerian EMCS Jun 2026]
  • Governance complexity and IDR legacy. The General Partner earns Incentive Distribution Rights (IDRs) that create a wedge between GP and LP interests. ET eliminated its IDRs in the 2019 simplification, but the governance perception discount from the 2020 Preferred Unit controversy persists.
  • 3.05-turn valuation discount to C-Corps. As of June 2026, C-Corp midstream trades at 11.94x forward EBITDA vs. MLP at 8.89x. At ET's scale, this is approximately $66B of equity value permanently sitting in the discount. [Ext: ETFTrends Alerian EMCS Jun 2026]

The July 2 2026 Redomiciliation: Why ET Just Left Delaware

All four entities. Effective July 13 2026. This is not administrative housekeeping. It is a deliberate governance restructuring with a specific history behind it.

The announcement: On July 2 2026, Energy Transfer LP, Sunoco LP, SunocoCorp LLC, and USA Compression Partners LP jointly announced redomiciliation from the State of Delaware to the State of Texas. Effective date: July 6 2026 legally, July 13 2026 for NYSE market purposes. CUSIPs, ticker symbols, and unitholder economic and governance rights are all preserved unchanged. [Ext: ET BusinessWire Jul 2 2026]

What the press release does not say: the real reasons

The announcement gives no strategic rationale beyond the legal mechanics. That omission is deliberate. The reasons are understood by every institutional investor and do not require explanation. Here they are in order of importance. [Ext: Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance DExit analysis; Jackson Walker LLP analysis; DEXIT trend reporting Jun 2026]

Reason 1: The Williams $410M verdict

This is the dominant reason. The Delaware Court of Chancery ordered ET to pay Williams Companies a $410 million breakup fee after ruling that ET breached its merger agreement and that ET's affirmative defenses were unconvincing. The judge found that ET issued a preferred offering that favored Kelcy Warren and other insiders in breach of operating covenants. That verdict was issued in Delaware. It was adjudicated by Delaware Chancery. The legal analysis that allowed that finding was built on decades of Delaware corporate law precedent specifically developed to protect minority investors in controlled companies. [Ext: Texas Lawbook Jan 2022; Delaware Supreme Court opinion]

ET has now moved all four entities out of Delaware's jurisdiction. Future litigation involving these entities will be adjudicated under Texas law, not Delaware law. Texas statutory law is more favorable to controlled companies. The analysis found that DEXIT businesses said other state statutes minimize director liability whereas Delaware's legal environment encourages unmeritorious and costly litigation against controlled companies. ET has faced that environment directly. It lost a $410M verdict in it. [Ext: Harvard Law School Corporate Governance Forum]

Reason 2: Kelcy Warren's controlled company exposure

Kelcy Warren co-founded Energy Transfer in 1996 and controls the General Partner. The 2020 Preferred Unit controversy, where ET issued convertible preferred units structured to protect Warren personally while common unitholders absorbed the distribution cut, was litigated in Delaware. Delaware's Court of Chancery is specifically equipped to scrutinize decisions by controlling shareholders and GPs that benefit insiders at the expense of public unitholders. That is its institutional specialization. Texas's Business Court, established in 2024, has far less precedent in this area and applies rules more favorable to the controlling party. Texas statutory law minimizes director liability in ways Delaware law does not. [Ext: Texas Lawbook; Delaware Chancery opinions; Harvard Law Corporate Governance]

Reason 3: Delaware franchise tax elimination

Delaware charges substantial franchise taxes on registered entities. One analysis compared a $250,000+ annual Delaware franchise tax to a $500 annual Texas business license fee. Across four entities (ET, SUN, SUNC, USAC) with significant registered agent and compliance costs, the annual savings are real but not large relative to $18B of EBITDA. This is a supporting reason, not the primary driver. [Ext: Jackson Walker LLP analysis; Wyoming News Now]

Reason 4: Texas DEXIT momentum and the new Business Court

Texas established a new Business Court in 2024 that began hearing complex commercial cases. Companies including ExxonMobil, Tesla, Dell Technologies, and Coinbase have already redomiciled from Delaware to Texas. ET, headquartered in Dallas, joins a wave of Texas-based companies making this move. The Texas Business Organizations Code was amended in 2024 and 2025 to make Texas more attractive for complex partnership structures. The timing is not coincidence. This is available infrastructure ET is using. [Ext: DExit trend reporting; Jackson Walker LLP; Harvard Law Forum]
